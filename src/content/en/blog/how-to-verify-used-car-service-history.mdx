# How to Verify a Used Car's Service History

A thick folder of paperwork feels reassuring, but volume is not the same as proof. Good service history forms a consistent timeline between mileage, dates, workshops, inspections, parts, and the condition of the car.

Verification matters particularly on German cars where gearbox fluid, timing components, cooling systems, diesel emissions hardware, and specialist repairs can change ownership risk dramatically.

## Build a mileage timeline

Put every invoice, inspection, and registration record in date order. Write down the mileage from each document and look for gaps, reversals, or implausibly small increases.

A gap is not automatically fraud. The car may have been stored, serviced without paperwork, or moved between countries. It is still an unknown that should affect your inspection and price.

## Read the work description

An invoice should say more than “full service.” Look for oil specification, filter part numbers, gearbox procedure, coolant type, brake-fluid work, and the exact components replaced.

Distinguish between:

- Recommended work
- Inspected items
- Parts supplied
- Work actually completed
- Temporary diagnosis or fault clearing

The [service invoice guide](/blog/how-to-read-a-used-car-service-invoice) explains how these phrases differ.

## Confirm the workshop exists

Check the workshop name, address, tax or registration details, and contact information. With the seller's permission, ask whether the workshop can confirm the invoice number and vehicle visit.

Do not expect a garage to release a previous owner's personal data. You only need confirmation that the document and work are genuine.

## Compare paper and digital records

Some newer cars store service entries digitally. Dealer or specialist systems may show visits that do not appear in the paper folder.

Digital history is useful but not infallible. Independent work may be missing, entries may be incomplete, and a dashboard service reset proves only that somebody reset the counter.

<Callout type="warn" title="A reset is not a service">
A fresh service indicator does not prove that the correct oil, filters, plugs, or gearbox work were completed.
</Callout>

## Match repairs to the physical car

If an invoice claims new brakes, inspect pad thickness, disc condition, date markings, and corrosion. If it claims a recent battery, check the battery label and registration data. If it claims four new tires, confirm matching date codes and tread.

The goal is not to accuse the seller. It is to see whether documents, component condition, and diagnostic data tell the same story.

## Check recurring faults

Repeated invoices for the same symptom can reveal incomplete diagnosis. Several coolant top-ups may matter more than one water-pump replacement. Repeated DPF regeneration work may indicate an unresolved thermostat, injector, EGR, or driving-pattern problem.

Look for continuity after a major repair. A timing-chain invoice is stronger when it lists the kit, guides, tensioner, seals, oil, and workshop warranty.

## Verify manufacturer campaigns

Ask a dealer or authorized service source whether recalls and service campaigns are outstanding for the VIN. Recall status can change by market, production date, and vehicle configuration.

## Buying decision

Strong history should be specific, chronological, and consistent with the car. Weak history relies on stamps without invoices, vague cash receipts, unexplained gaps, or claims that cannot be matched to installed parts.

Use the [buying checklist](/buying-checklist) to combine history with a cold start, diagnostic scan, road test, and physical inspection. Paperwork reduces uncertainty; it never replaces checking the vehicle itself.
