# How Fuel Trims Reveal Vacuum Leaks Before You Buy

A modern petrol engine constantly adjusts injection to keep the mixture near its target. Short-term fuel trim reacts quickly; long-term trim records the correction learned over time. Together they can reveal unmetered air even when no hose is visibly split.

## Compare idle with raised rpm

Warm the engine fully with major electrical loads off. Record short- and long-term trim at idle, then hold roughly 2,000 to 2,500 rpm. A strongly positive correction at idle that improves as airflow increases often points toward a vacuum, PCV, intake-gasket, or leaking purge-valve problem.

Positive correction that remains high under load can instead suggest weak fuel delivery, incorrect airflow measurement, or an exhaust leak affecting sensor feedback. One number is not enough; the pattern is the evidence.

<Callout type="warn" title="Do not diagnose from trim alone">
Check both banks where applicable and consider coolant temperature, oxygen-sensor operation, misfire counters, and recently cleared adaptations.
</Callout>

## Listen and test

Whistling, a rough idle, oil-cap suction, or idle speed changing when the cap is loosened can support a crankcase-ventilation fault. The decisive check is usually a correctly regulated smoke test. Excess pressure can damage seals, so use suitable equipment.

## Used-car decision

Small hoses may be inexpensive, but integrated PCV covers, buried manifold gaskets, and neglected lean running can cost much more. Scan before and after the test drive, compare trims at several operating points, and insist on diagnosis when P0171, P2187, or repeated misfires are present.
