# Why a Pre-Purchase Scan Is Worth It

A car can look clean, drive well, and still carry expensive stored faults.

That is why a diagnostic scan is one of the cheapest forms of protection before buying a used German car. It turns hidden electronic history into something you can discuss, price, or reject before money changes hands.

## What a scan can reveal

A good scan can show:

- Stored engine and gearbox faults
- Emissions system warnings
- Sensor plausibility issues
- Misfire history
- Battery voltage problems
- Recently cleared codes
- Module communication errors

<Callout type="tip" title="Scan after the test drive">
The best scan happens after a proper road test. Some faults only return once the car is warm, under boost, shifting repeatedly, regenerating, or moving through traffic.
</Callout>

## Generic OBD vs proper module scan

A cheap generic OBD reader can be useful, but it usually sees only part of the car. Many German cars store important faults in gearbox, ABS, body, steering, suspension, infotainment, climate, parking, and emissions modules.

For a pre-purchase inspection, you want a tool that can read the whole vehicle. The goal is not to collect codes for fun. The goal is to see whether the seller's story matches the car's stored history.

## What a scan cannot prove

A scan is not magic. It does not replace a mechanical inspection, test drive, leak check, tire inspection, or service history review.

It also cannot guarantee a part will not fail next week. A clean scan is a strong signal, not a warranty. Some mechanical issues are visible, audible, or smell-based rather than electronic.

## How to read the result

Do not panic at every code. Some faults are old, low priority, or voltage related. The important part is pattern recognition.

Pay attention to:

- Current faults, not just historic faults
- Repeated faults in the same system
- Gearbox, transfer case, or AWD faults
- Misfire counters or cylinder-specific patterns
- AdBlue, NOx, DPF, EGR, or emissions readiness issues
- Low-voltage faults across many modules
- Codes cleared shortly before viewing

If many modules show low voltage, the car may simply need a battery and charging-system check. If the engine and gearbox show active faults after a road test, slow down.

## Recently cleared codes

Freshly cleared codes do not always mean fraud. A garage may clear codes after repair. But if the seller cannot explain why codes were cleared, or if warning lights return during the test drive, treat that as a serious signal.

Ask for repair invoices that match the cleared codes. If the story is vague, the scan has done its job: it showed you where to be careful.

<Callout type="warn" title="Refusing a scan is information">
If a seller blocks a reasonable scan before purchase, you do not need to argue. You can simply price the car as unknown or walk away.
</Callout>

## How to use the result

Turn the scan into buying action:

- Clean scan plus strong service history: continue inspection
- Minor historic codes with explanation: negotiate normally
- Current emissions or gearbox faults: request diagnosis before purchase
- Cleared codes with no explanation: treat as high uncertainty
- Seller refuses scan: walk away or price aggressively

A scan does not make the decision for you. It gives the decision better evidence.
